A codebase is a city

Population: 60,000 sloc

Posted on Nov 20, 2017 2 mins read

When I was eight years old my family moved from Salt Lake City to the Utah/Arizona border. My dad wanted to get away from the snow. Our new house was an suburban three-story that was taller than it was wide, part of a neighborhood whose streets were curling rays extending outward from a middle-class golf course.

Any kid with a bike knows what to do in a new neighborhood. I wheeled out on evenings and weekends and learned the streets. It wasn’t long before I could call up a mental map from point A to anywhere. All the same, for years to come I would occasionally discover cul-de-sacs or trails I’d never noticed before. There’s nothing quite like chasing a bird around an unfamiliar bend and across a vacant lot, and gradually realizing that you know where you are, but you’ve passed through a wormhole and your whole concept of Bloomington Drive has been bent in half.

I grew up without Google Maps. My future kids will wonder what that’s like. I got lost a lot.

Every city has a downtown. When you move into a new city, it takes a few days or weeks to figure out where you are relative to it. I mean, you know cardinally where you’re at. But that doesn’t count for anything until you know how to go “the back way” into town, how to escape rush hour traffic or where the best view is.

Getting into a new codebase is like that. Right?

Heaven help you if you’ve moved into the software equivalent of Delhi or New York City, sprawling metropolises with 20 million lines of code or more. I’ve never spent more than a few days in a place like that. I imagine your first move is to find something you can fix, something that isn’t hopelessly interconnected with everything else. Something with inputs and outputs you can understand. You spend some time there and then you start following alleyways outward, expanding your perspective a little bit each month. You can’t learn the whole city. You’ll run out of RAM. Once you know enough to be useful, you’ll start forgetting as much as you learn.

I’m more at home with the frontier capitals and lakeside college towns, the 50,000-line codebases that have only recently spawned their own zoning committees and city councils. A codebase like that can still change directions. A person with the right dream can make it big.
