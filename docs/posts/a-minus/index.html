Self

I'm not an "A player"

Posted on Jul 7, 2022 2 mins read

When I look back on my life, a running theme is the steady stream of A-minuses.

I’m almost great at a lot of things. I almost placed in a “for children, by children” book writing contest when I was a kid. I almost got a 4.0 GPA in high school. I almost got the highest possible score on the ACT. I almost made it to nationals in speech and debate.

I took an IQ test last year as part of a neurological panel. I don’t put a lot of stock in IQ. Still, I was amused by the results: smart, but not the smartest person you know. Somewhere up there in the “almost smart enough to be useful” zone.

I averaged A minuses in college. Even my blood type is A negative.

These are just a few items from a very long list. But in general, I’m like 10% short of awesome. I realize it’s all relative. I sound like a whiner. But I’m not a whiner, I’m a twenty-something in recovery from lifelong perfectionism.

When I was getting ready to go to college, my debate coach—a trusted friend and mentor—gave me some advice. “Don’t worry about your grades,” he said. “Do enough to get by, but don’t stress about getting A’s. The unhappiest people I knew in college were the ones always worked up about their grades and their scholarship.”

I made no effort whatsoever to listen to him. I spent four years doing homework day and night. I worried constantly about my grades. I set up three or four different systems to make sure I never missed a deadline. By and large I was miserable. I can’t change who I was, so I can’t change what I did. But I wish I could have gone about it differently.
