On the common cold

Posted on Mar 22, 2022 3 mins read

My immune system is a little skewampus. I catch way more colds than the statistics say I should. It’s not a low white blood cell count—I’ve had that checked out because it’s what got my grandpa in the end (granted, he lived a long life). It’s not smoking, since I don’t smoke. I don’t think it’s allergies either. How many times can a person get allergies in a year? I don’t really know what it is, but it annoys me to no end.

I hate colds. Some of it’s physical, some of it’s emotional. I got colds all the time when I was a kid. The head-cold restrictions at my house were miserable: no sugar, no friends, no Tylenol or decongestants or meds of any kind, no doctors, no sympathy, no reprieve from the chore chart. If you had a cold, you took some sketchy herbal supplements and powered through. For me the routine was very familiar: first a sore throat, often very raw and excruciating. Then a runny nose that required constant attention. Finally, a wet cough that persisted for several days. I’d feel better for a couple weeks, a month if I was lucky, and then repeat the whole process again.

The greatest increases in my quality of life have been as follows:

  1. When I graduated from college. Much less time around strangers and therefore fewer colds.
  2. When I started working from home. No more open floor plan (🤮), less time around coworkers, and fewer colds.
  3. The COVID-19 pandemic. The only good thing about the pandemic was that I didn’t catch cold for a year. The masks, the Zoom meetings, and the public gathering restrictions kept me happy and healthy in a way I’d never been. I may well be a grocery store masker for the rest of my life.

I understand colds aren’t a big deal for some people. I don’t get it. Your face and lungs turn into a damned swamp and you just shrug it off? Sounds fake. Sure, there are worse things in life. I’ve had food poisoning. I’ve walked on a broken foot. I got a mono-and-strep combo in college that just about killed me. And a lot of people have chronic conditions that make the common cold look like a hangnail. But still: if I have a cold, it defines my existence until it goes away. Sense of humor, gone. Life satisfaction, gone. Ability to get things done, drastically reduced. Colds suck.

So, obviously, I’m not impressed when someone uses “the common cold” as a rhetorical metaphor for something harmless and ignorable.

Quick poll: if you knew you were going to catch cold next week but you could pay to prevent it, how much would you be willing to pay? 25 dollars? A hundred? A thousand? I think I’m comfortably in the hundred-dollar range, offhand. But if a cold reduces my work capacity by 50% for one week, a thousand dollars isn’t an overtly unreasonable price.

On the other hand, sometimes I’m relieved in spite of myself when I catch cold. Sometimes I’ve been working too hard for too long and a cold seems to be my body’s way of saying take a break. For the first day or two it’s nice to shift to a lower gear, put off the to-do list, and take some time off work. It’s miserable after that, of course, but it starts out feeling like a forced vacation. And it’s one I don’t have to feel guilty about. No one can blame me for getting a cold.

So yeah, there are worse things than colds. I’ve never felt relieved because my bank account was at zero or because I was in a fight with someone I love. Some things are all downside.
