On identity and belief

Posted on Jun 20, 2022 5 mins read

Identity is a subject of misunderstanding in modern discourse. There’s constant talk of “identity politics,” a term that both contradicts itself and erases issues of real identity, and efforts are being made to equate identity, the facts of your personhood, with belief, the suppositions you make about meaning and destiny in relation to yourself, other people, and the universe.

A few important differences between identity and belief are:

  1. While beliefs may relate to people and objects outside the self, identity does not and cannot cross outside the boundary of a person’s own body. My identity has nothing to do with you. Your identity has nothing to do with me.
  2. Beliefs are formed by interaction and observation. Identity is not created by influence—it may respond to an environment, but it belongs profoundly and completely to the person that bears it. Race, gender, sexuality, body shape, and personal experience are all part of one’s identity.
  3. It’s rare for a person to be able to consciously change their identity (which isn’t to say they can’t discover something new about their identity or choose to reveal a piece that they’ve kept hidden) but beliefs can change at will.
  4. Every person has an absolute right to their identity at all times. It’s never wrong for your identity to be visible. It’s impossible for your identity to infringe on another person’s rights. Beliefs, on the other hand, can infringe. It’s not appropriate to have your beliefs on display in certain places even if you’re otherwise allowed to be there. Some beliefs aren’t appropriate to hold at all.
  5. All identities are morally equal. Beliefs, however, are morally disaparate. A belief in the worth and value of every human identity is morally superior to the belief that some identities are evil or disgusting.

I’ve already noted some of the major components of identity. Let’s give some examples of belief. Belief includes your politics, religion, media preferences, philosophy, and occupation. This is why “identity politics” is a contradiction in terms. Your politics aren’t part of your identity. “Religious identity” is also a misguided term. Your current religious beliefs aren’t your identity, as strongly as you may feel about them.

There are some things that blur the boundary between identity and belief, of course. Your life experience may be strongly affected by a religious upbringing. You could change what you believe but those experiences would always be part of your identity. Then again, we rarely think about our experiences in terms of raw facts—we typically frame them as stories, and stories are architected by beliefs. As another example, your likes and dislikes typically aren’t things you choose; apples are delicious is a belief but I’m a person who enjoys apples has a strain of identity in it.

Why is it useful to draw lines between identity and belief? For one thing, it helps us distinguish between the discomfort of moral growth and the pain of a genuine attack. We are not harmed when our beliefs change, so when we encounter new information or see a change in social norms, we can be reasonably expected to abandon any invalidated beliefs; the alternative is to blind ourselves to real events. But since identities are inextricable from selves, any argument against an identity is necessarily an attempt to harm or silence its bearer.

Sometimes a plea for tolerance is taken as hypocritical because it includes identities and not beliefs. There is no hypocrisy in this. We can tolerate (and even appreciate) all identities while excluding and stigmatizing certain beliefs—and we must do so in order for a community to function. Identities are harmless and victimless; beliefs can injure and destroy.

Again, the confusion between identity and belief will trip us up here. One may say they “identify as a white supremacist,” but such a statement is so deeply invalid it’s a wonder it can even be phrased grammatically. Racial supremacy is a belief, not an identity. It’s something that can be claimed or disclaimed by any person at any time. Whether it is held extremely dear, whether one feels emotionally attached to it, whether they measure the world with it, is irrelevant. A belief, like an article of clothing, is completely detachable from a person. Any resistance they feel to the process is vanity.

It’s equally ridiculous for one to say that they “believe” themself to be white, assuming their ancestry is not in question. Race can’t always be ascertained at a glance, but it is immutable. Some other characteristics, such as gender identity and sexuality, may (uncommonly) change over the course of a lifetime, but at any given moment they are also immutable; you can’t be anything other than what you are, and you can’t change your mind about it, other than to obscure or reveal it.

One discomfort that arises from all this is the implication that morally virtuous beliefs are neither innate nor bound to the self. This is for the best. The same threads that would bind virtue to the self would also introduce the possibility of binding corruption. If these threads existed, we’d be obligated to cut them by any means necessary so that no human is irredeemable. But as far as the evidence is concerned, they do not. Beliefs may be very deeply ingrained but they are not part of the body.
