A bit of idle philosophy

Unit testing our ethics

Posted on Aug 17, 2020 2 mins read

I’ve been reading some philosophy lately. Specifically: stoicism, taoism, and Western ethics. I took a Kierkegaard class and some literary theory in college, but found it all a little dense and boring back then, so this is the first time I’ve returned to such readings.

I have one criticism of Western philosophy (which may well be the product of not having studied it in greater depth). It is this: philosophers seem determined to oversimplify each concept they encounter well beyond the point of relevance in a nuanced world, and then use the greatest complexity they can summon in debating it. I will allow Immanuel Kant to argue my defense, from his Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals:

Act only according to that maxim whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law.

In other words, when faced with a moral decision, you should only do whatever you believe everybody should do in that situation. While I think this is a decent guideline for some decisions, such as whether to vote or not, its universal prescriptiveness seems destined to fail in a world of constant moral tradeoffs. When was the last time you had an unambiguous moral decision to make and complete information about it?

We usually aren’t deciding whether or not to kill or torture people. We’re deciding if we should buy our grandma a smartphone (mining of rare metals, supporting megacorporations that exploit workers, polluting the Earth with plastics…and giving grandma a way to stay in touch during the final years of her life). We’re deciding if we should scold our racist uncle on Facebook (he’s never gonna change his mind, and the ensuing argument will touch off an anxiety spiral that may last the rest of the day…but maybe it will help someone else form a better opinion?). We’re deciding if we should have a Facebook account at all (giving up privacy to a company that’s more than happy to sell out its users and feels no responsibility to correct disinformation…and staying in touch with people we don’t talk to any other way). Whatever decision we make, we may or may not be sure enough about it to “will that it should become a universal law.”
