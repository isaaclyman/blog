The illusion of social media

Posted on Feb 13, 2024 3 mins read

Like many of my generation, I spend too much time scrolling through social media. By now it’s cliché to say social media is addictive (an ill-defined term at best), but the problem is subtler and more complex than that. Potato chips are also labeled “addictive”, sometimes for marketing purposes and sometimes as part of a larger moral panic about food, but at least with potato chips you know what you’re getting: deep-fried potatoes and salt. Frito-Lay spends very little time trying to convince you otherwise. Social media, on the other hand, both actively and subconsciously tries to convince you it can fulfill core human needs. While this isn’t entirely false, the illusion far outpaces reality.

Social media promises an emotional experience of rest, excitement, accomplishment, connection, narrative, and learning. It rarely follows through.

The illusion of rest

Social media has embedded itself in the modern lifestyle as a way to unwind. It holds this position by virtue of ultra-low activation energy: you’ve got your phone on you anyway, so objects of mild interest and amusement are only a couple of taps away. There’s no inherent time commitment and no effort required. Scrolling through your feed while sprawled on the couch certainly feels like resting.

But you’re practically guaranteed to finish your scrolling session just as exhausted as you were when you started. Social media takes as much as it gives: some of your feed is humor and art, some is rage and disregard for human life, some is callous advertising. Even when it’s mostly positive, the shallowness and constant shifting of attention is draining. It’s a continous drip-feed of just enough mental stimulation to get you from one post to the next. It’s a closed loop. If it energized you too much or too little, you’d do something else.

This presents us with the uncomfortable fact that genuine rest requires intent and investment. It’s actually harder to read a book, watch television, meditate, or take a nap than look at social media. All of those things involve decision-making and the prospect of disappointment. Social media, on the other hand, doesn’t ask us to choose—the feed only goes one direction—and if something disappoints us, only a few seconds are wasted; a logically distinct experience is only a finger-swipe away.

Social media promises rest, but can only provide stasis.

The illusion of excitement

Life is the same more often than different; it’s boring more often than exciting. Each new thing becomes old before we’ve had our fill. Social media promises a virtual world where something new and exciting is always happening—a cure for ennui.
