Self

Modes of knowledge and communication

Posted on Feb 21, 2022 4 mins read

I’ve been thinking about the reasons we communicate with each other: what we are doing when we use words in the presence of a person. A related subject is how we come to know information—epistemology, that is—and why we know (or will ourselves to know) what we know. Below is an outline of ways different types of information, knowledge and communication could be represented.

Types of information
  • Information that is verifiable.
    • Example: There is a car in my garage.
    • The earth is flat. (This is a verifiable statement. It has been verified to be false.)
    • Nietzsche wrote 15 books.
  • Information that is not coherent with the idea of verification.
    • Jack and Jill went up the hill to fetch a pail of water.
    • I am in love with her.
    • Salted vanilla is the best flavor of ice cream.

I’ve wondered whether there is information that is coherent with the idea of verification, but not verifiable. Take the famous example of Russell’s Teapot, in which an ordinary teapot is asserted to be in orbit around the sun, too small for telescopes to observe and with an unknown location and trajectory. No one could say with absolute certainty that the teapot does or does not exist. The analogy is provocative but falls outside the scope of what I’m interested in here. A spacefaring teapot belongs in the first category: if we had the technology we could absolutely determine whether it was there or not.

In short I am saying that with sufficient access to means of observation, everything is either verifiable or completely outside the question of objective truth. It’s potentially a bold statement. I am lucky it is not verifiable.

Modes of knowledge
  • Knowing what is immediate (i.e. through the senses).
  • Knowing what is given without self-interest.
  • Knowing by means of desirability or convenience.
  • Knowing by means of group norms.

“Given without self-interest” is difficult phrasing. What I mean is that some types of information do not try to persuade us. There is no emotional benefit to believing or disbelieving. Very little of what you can learn from a math textbook has any social considerations; you do not cling to special relativity because it gets you through hard times. These facts are too boring for controversy, and yet they aren’t immediately observable.

The last two modes of knowledge may also need some explanation. “Knowing by means of desirability or convenience” refers to propositions that we “know” because they make life less gloomy, like “there’s someone out there for everyone.” And “knowing by means of group norms” refers to things we “know” as a way of enacting the rules of groups we perceive ourselves to be part of—political beliefs being perhaps the easiest example, though religious and socioeconomic groups also fit here.

These modes of knowledge don’t map cleanly to one or the other type of information.

  • Immediate: We know through our senses that the sky is blue or the floor is hard (verifiable statements), but we also know through our senses that ice cream is good and water chestnuts are bad (not coherent with verification).
  • Given: We are given Newton’s laws in school (verifiable) but we are also given jokes and tall tales (not verifiable).
  • Convenience: We know that when you wish on a birthday candle, your wish comes true (verifiably false) and we also know that one’s best is always good enough (not verifiable).
  • Group norms: We know that a certain politician has cheated on their spouse (verifiable), and we know the opposition party wants to ruin the country (not verifiable).
Modes of communication

I use the word “speak” here in a traditional sense referring to all communication between people, whether spoken aloud, written, signed, or shrugged.

  • We speak to communicate information.
    • The bus is coming in ten minutes.
  • We speak to perform an act.
    • I now pronounce you man and wife.
  • We speak to preserve the self.
    • That’s so funny! You’re funny.

Most communication accomplishes two of these or even all three, but I posit that by far the most important one is the third. We put the most effort into it, we dedicate the highest percentage of our communication to it, and it is the only mode of communication we are innately comfortable with on its own.
