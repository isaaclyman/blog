The Myth of Sisyphus by Albert Camus: A digest

Terms, chapter summaries, and highlights

Posted on Feb 17, 2022 11 mins read

The Myth of Sisyphus is a 160-page essay by French-Algerian philosopher Albert Camus (Albert like “owlbear”, Camus like “kamoo”). It frames his philosophy of absurdism, the simultaneous acceptance of and rebellion against the fact that the universe has no inherent meaning or organizing principle.

Terms

  • Absurd: “The absurd” refers to “the absurdity of man’s existence.” Camus asserts that humans are hard-wired to seek meaning in the universe, but the universe has no inherent meaning. Humanity, then, strives toward a goal that can’t be achieved. We do this even if we know the goal can’t be achieved. This is absurd. Camus doesn’t use the word “absurd” to ridicule the human condition, only to describe it.
  • Absurd man: Camus’s philosophical ideal; a person who maintains a constant awareness of the absurd and doesn’t try to escape it.
  • Antinomy: Paradox. When two ideas contradict, but not necessarily in a way that makes one of them false.
  • Consciousness: See Lucidity.
  • Desert: A metaphor for the absence of meaning in the universe.
  • Eternal: The “eternal” is the idea of something beyond death, something that makes our actions take on grander significance than they perhaps deserve. See also Hope.
  • Hope: The unfounded belief that our actions have significance outside of themselves, that they may tip scales or affect prospects beyond their immediate, physical effects. Belief in god or an afterlife is a type of hope.
  • Humiliated: Seems to refer to someone/something whose concept of reason and purpose in the universe has been destroyed. Camus attributes this terminology to Heidegger, but it doesn’t appear that Heidegger said anything of the sort.
  • Leap: From Kierkegaard’s “leap of faith.” The irrational choice one must make to believe in something eternal or transcendent. This escapes the absurd, but at great cost.
  • Live without appeal: To assess one’s life in terms of itself, without some grander scale to judge it on. That is, without appealing to a higher power or purpose to validate one’s choices.
  • Lucidity: An awareness of the absurd.
  • Negate: To ignore or imagine away one of the premises of the absurd. See Leap.
  • Nostalgia: Wistfulness for the days when one believed in the eternal, before confrontation with the absurd.
  • Phenomenology: The philosophical study of experience and consciousness. Phenomenologists describe life in terms of sensory experiences or phenomena.
  • Revolt: The essential thing keeping the conscious person from giving up. We revolt against the absurd by recognizing it, accepting it, refusing to escape it, acknowledging that our efforts are hopeless and fleeting, and yet continuing to live and strive. It is the struggle between ourselves and the absurd that makes life good.
  • Sterile: Without purpose, without an end in mind. A sterile action can be fully assessed in and of itself, not as a step along some greater path.

Chapter summaries and quotes

TW: Abstract discussion of suicide. Note that Camus and his philosophy of absurdism are explicitly and repeatedly anti-suicide.

An Absurd Reasoning

Absurdity and Suicide

Camus asserts that suicide—the question of whether life is worth living—is the only serious philosophical question, that all others pale in comparison.

I have never seen anyone die for the ontological argument. (4)

The “ontological argument” is an abstract argument for the existence of god involving the nature of imagination. Camus uses it as an example of something that is clearly less important than the question of suicide.

Living, naturally, is never easy. […] Dying voluntarily implies that you have recognized […] the absence of any profound reason for living, the insane character of that daily agitation, and the uselessness of suffering. […] This divorce between man and his life, the actor and his setting, is properly the feeling of absurdity. (5-6)

Here Camus introduces the feeling of absurdity, the understanding that life has no meaning and yet we all feel a desperate need for one.

[…] for a man who does not cheat, what he believes to be true must determine his action. Belief in the absurdity of existence must then dictate his conduct. It is legitimate to wonder […] whether a conclusion of this importance requires forsaking as rapidly as possible an incomprehensible condition. (7)

This is the big question of the first part of the book: if the absurd is so depressing, should we just get rid of it? Should we take a “leap of faith” and believe in god, for example, or simply ignore the conditions of absurdity? Why would we accept something that makes us feel despondent?

Hitherto […] people have played on words and pretended to believe that refusing to grant a meaning to life necessarily leads to declaring that it is not worth living. In truth, there is no necessary common measure between these two judgments. (8)

This foreshadows Camus’s later conclusion, that the meaninglessness of life is not necessarily a bad thing, and doesn’t make it something to throw away.

Absurd Walls

[Man] situates himself in realtion to time. […] He admits that he stands at a certain point on a curve that he acknowledges having to travel to its end. He belongs to time, and by the horror that seizes him, he recognizes his worst enemy. Tomorrow, he was longing for tomorrow, whereas everything in him ought to reject it. (14)

We tend to live in the future, chasing after that “next thing”—graduation, a higher salary, a family—even though the approach of the future leads inevitably to death. The acceptance of one’s own mortality can be staggering, as well the realization that life is fleeting and everything we do or create will pass away in time.

[…] one will never be sufficiently surprised that everyone lives as if no one “knew.” This is because in reality there is no experience in death. […] it is barely possible to speak of the experience of others’ deaths. It is a substitute, an illusion, and it never quite convinces us. (15)

This is perhaps the most common response to the absurd and specifically the inevitability of death: to pretend it doesn’t exist. We don’t really know what death is like and others’ deaths never really convince us that our own is coming.

From the moment absurdity is recognized, it becomes a passion, the most harrowing of all. (22)

Camus believes that once you see the absurd you can’t un-see it. It bothers you all the time.

If one could only say just once: “This is clear,” all would be saved. (27)

If there were one single transcendent truth that we could know for certain, the absurd would be vanquished. But philosophers have done a bang-up job proving that we’re not really certain of anything.

[Man] feels within him his longing for happiness and for reason. The absurd is born of this confrontation between the human need and the unreasonable silence of the world. (28)

Philosophical Suicide

If I attempt to solve a problem, at least I must not by that very solution conjure away one of the terms of the problem. (31)

Camus begins his argument against various forms of hope and faith. He opposes them because they ignore the facts he feels he has proven beyond question. In other words, faith is not a “solution” to the absurd; it is an avoidance of it.

[…] without justification, as [man] says to himself, he suddenly asserts all at once the transcendent, the essence of experience, and the superhuman significance of life when he writes: “Does not the failure reveal, beyond any possible explanation and interpretation, not the absence but the existence of transcendence?” […] Thus the absurd becomes god (in the broadest meaning of this word) and that inability to understand becomes the existence that illuminates everything. (32-33)

We turn toward God only to obtain the impossible. (34)

Camus brings up Kierkegaard and Chestov, two religious philosophers, and accuses them of worshipping (in modern terms) a “god of the gaps”—a god that people say must exist because there are facts science cannot explain. A lot of things don’t make sense, and some people take this as proof of a higher power. To Camus, this is a ridiculous non sequitur. All forms of existentialism seem to him religious and opposed to reason.

Everything is sacrificed here to the irrational, and, the demand for clarity being conjured away, the absurd disappears […] The absurd man, on the other hand, does not undertake such a leveling process. He recognizes the struggle, does not absolutely scorn reason, and admits the irrational. Thus he again embraces in a single glance all the data of experience and he is little inclined to leap before knowing. (37)

[…] what Kierkegaard calls for quite plainly is […] “The sacrifice of the intellect.” (38)

When we accept faith as a substitute for the absurd, we are giving up clarity, sacrificing our intellect, and defeating our own pursuit of truth.

The important thing […] is not to be cured, but to live with one’s ailments. (38)

I want to know whether I can live with what I know and with that alone. (40)

It is a matter of living in that state of the absurd. […] this mind and this world straining against each other without being able to embrace each other. I ask for the rule of life in that state[.] (40-41)

This is second big question of the book. If one has opted to live with the absurd rather than tossing it away, how can it be done?

Seeking what is true is not seeking what is desirable. If in order to elude the anxious qauestion: “What would life be?” one must, like the donkey, feed on the roses of illusion, then the absurd mind, rather than resigning itself to falsehood, prefers to adopt fearlessly Kierkegaard’s reply: “despair.” (41)

The terms of Camus’s question are set out. He would rather be sad than live a false life. If living with the absurd means living in despair, too bad. (Spoiler alert: it doesn’t.)

Reason and the irrational lead to the same preaching. […] The abstract philosopher and the religious philosopher start out from the same disorder and support each other in the same anxiety. […] For [the absurd mind] the world is neither so irrational nor so irrational. It is unreasonable and only that. (48-49)

Camus takes a moment to defend absurdism against rationalism—that is, those who believe that everything can be explained by reason and scientific inquiry. Science may tell us how, but it doesn’t tell us why, and therefore has limits. To ignore those limits is just as much a leap of faith as the religious philosophies mentioned above.

Absurd Freedom

I don’t know whether this world has a meaning that transcends it. But I know that I do not know that meaning and that it is impossible for me just now to know it. What can a meaning outside my condition mean to me? I can understand only in human terms. What I touch, what resists me—that is what I understand. (51)

Importantly, Camus places limits on his own philosophy: he doesn’t know whether there is meaning in the universe or not, and he does not know whether it will be possible at another time to know, but he must construct his philosophy as though it will not be, since it doesn’t seem that humanity’s efforts up to this point have yielded anything. He also places limits on meanings that might be suggested: if they don’t fall into the realm of things he can touch or observe, they will mean nothing to him.

At a certain point on his path the absurd man is tempted. History is not lacking in either religions or prophets, even without gods. He is asked to leap. All he can reply is that he doesn’t fully understand, that it is not obvious. Indeed, he does not want to do anything but what he fully understands. (52-53)

This is one of many times Camus admits the appeal of faith. It is extremely tempting to accept some transcendent power that gives life meaning. But this would be doing something without fully understanding it—accepting something outside of one’s own condition.

It was previously a question of finding out whether or not life had to have a meaning to be lived. It now becomes clear, on the contrary, that it will be lived all the better if it has no meaning. Living an experience, a particular fate, is accepting it fully. Now, no one will live this fate, knowing it to be absurd, unless he does everything to keep before him that absurd brought to light by consciousness. (53)

This seems to me the turning point of the book. “It now becomes clear” is perhaps premature, as many pages will yet be spent proving out this system of living. But the core of it is captured here: we can’t live fully in the present unless we deny the future. We can’t fully experience something unless we accept everything about it, including its meaninglessness. Therefore, living life to the fullest requires us to keep the absurd in our minds at all times.

Living is keeping the absurd alive. Keeping it alive is, above all, contemplating it. Unlike Eurydice, the absurd dies only when we turn away from it. One of the only coherent philosophical positions is thus revolt. It is a constant confrontation between man and his own obscurity. […] That revolt is the certainty of a crushing fate, without the resignation that ought to accompany it. (54)

The only way to keep living in the face of the absurd is to fight it. This is different from ignoring it or trying to disprove it. We are aware of it and do not believe it is resolvable, and yet we reject its promise of despair.

The absurd man can only drain everything to the bitter end, and deplete himself. The absurd is his extreme tension, which he maintains constantly by solitary effort, for he knows that in that consciousness and in that day-to-day revolt he gives proof of his only truth, which is defiance. (55)

Ooh, somebody’s got a rebellious streak!
