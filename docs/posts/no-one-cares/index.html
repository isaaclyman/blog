If you build it, no one will care

How it got like this

Posted on Oct 20, 2022 3 mins read

One of the most misleading quotes of all time comes from 1989 Academy Award nominee Field of Dreams. The original line is “If you build it, he will come,” and it’s delivered as a disembodied whisper that leads the main character to build a baseball stadium in the middle of nowhere, at great cost to himself and his family.

The line as everyone repeats it is “If you build it, they will come,” and it’s typically used to encourage someone to pursue a massive or noble project in spite of their doubts. The message is that if you build something truly excellent, people will materialize out of thin air to patronize it. This is false. It has always been false. The act of creation rarely draws attention, and even when it does, the nature and volume of the attention is uncorrelated with the quality of the project.

I won’t use my own work as proof because I would (rightly) be accused of letting my ego drive the assessment, but I could give examples at both ends—one or two throwaway projects that received significant attention for no good reason and several creations of blood, sweat, and tears that went completely ignored. Really, any creator of any stripe has stories to tell. You spend months making something perfect, obsessing over the details, pouring your soul into it, and then stand and watch as every human being in the universe steadfastly ignores it. You do this over and over again to prove it’s not a fluke. And eventually you realize: nobody cares.

How did we get like this? When one of my friends announces they’re starting a new project, I’m caught between two knee-jerk reactions: the desire to support them and be a good friend, and a sense of second-hand embarrassment. It’s anyone’s guess which reaction will win. That’s just Tom from high school, says the latter. He doesn’t know how to do stuff like this. He’s on an announcement high. It’ll burn out soon. And that ignore-it-until-it-goes-away impulse is proven right over and over again. Most people don’t try anything at all, and the ones who do inevitably fail. There are exceptions here and there—the class clown from my high school years has started two successful companies since we graduated—but for the most part, good ideas and hard work take you right back to where you started. It doesn’t matter how smart you are; unless you’re extraordinarily lucky, you’re going to die in the same socioeconomic bracket you were born into, with no legacy other than several decades of wage labor and perhaps a few kids.

  • You can’t get credibility with someone who knew you in high school.
  • Marketing is hard and constant work
  • No one buys things because they are valuable
  • Everyone is trying to get your attention, the only part of you they care about
  • Most of the things trying to get your attention are shitty
  • We’re playing a terrible cat-and-mouse game with advertisers, where they try to draw money out of our wallets more and more slowly, and we get more and more desensitized to straightforward value propositions

  • Capitalism sucks
