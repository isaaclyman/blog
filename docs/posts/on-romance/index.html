Self

On romance

and its components

Posted on Jan 2, 2018 5 mins read

It seems to me that there are four pillars of romantic life:

  1. Attraction
  2. Love (i.e. “in love,” not “love ya, mom”)
  3. Physical intimacy
  4. Commitment (e.g. exclusivity, marriage)

There’s a tendency to think of them as a package deal, but on deeper inspection, they’re clearly separate. Any of them can exist without the others.

  • Attraction only: A foundational human experience. You find a person attractive, but for reasons of decency, nerves, loyalty, intuition, or good judgment, you do nothing about it. You sit on it and wait for it to fizzle out.

    There are, of course, people who don’t experience attraction. I don’t know enough about asexuality to write about that.
  • Love only: This is a classic romantic-comedy staple. Alice is in love with Bob, her best friend, but Bob is engaged to Jane, but their relationship is rocky and Bob depends on Alice to get him through the hard times. Eventually, Bob realizes that Alice (whom he loves, thought he was slow to admit it) is better for him than Jane (who he is openly attracted to, intimate with, and committed to). Bob kisses Alice. Credits roll. There are more mundane examples of this, too, though they don’t often show up in popular culture. I suspect it’s hard to win an audience’s sympathies with an unglamorous tale of a person falling for someone who is unattractive to them.
  • Physical intimacy only: Almost everything in this section falls under “stuff my parents would be horrified to hear about on the evening news.” Prostitution, casual sex, and so forth.
  • Commitment only: I think we all know a couple that seems only to be married for the sake of not getting divorced. When attraction, love and intimacy die out, the relationship is bad by definition. A slightly less tragic example is the arranged betrothal common in some cultures; one hopes that attraction, love and intimacy will develop once the marriage is enacted, but there may be no opportunity until then.

None of these scenarios are what I daydreamed about when I was young and single. The first is inconvenient, the second is forlorn, the third is unsatisfying, and the last is suffocating.

So don’t pick one and make it your New Year’s resolution, eh?

Any combination of two or three pillars can also exist, to the exclusion of the other(s). I won’t walk you through all 10 combinations, but maybe you can take a moment to check my logic against your imagination.

My thesis ends here, but I have some observations and some questions I want to pose.

Observations

One of my college professors made the claim that all attraction is temporary, and all successful relationships have to transform over time so that they depend less on attraction and more on friendship. Although the implication makes me uncomfortable (that she is not attracted to her husband and hasn’t been for many years), maybe it shouldn’t.

My former religious leaders (and various sources of “pop wisdom”) taught that love is active, not passive. That is, falling in love and falling out of love are the predictable results of actions undertaken by one or both partners in a relationship. This casts doubt on the accuracy of the word “falling” in either phrase, given that technically, one cannot choose whether or not to fall. In any case, the actions purported to induce love are various forms of kindness, service, forgiveness, honesty, and thoughtfulness.

Consider that there may be dependencies at play. I believe that without commitment, love will eventually die. Commitment being the basis for continuous and thoughtful investment in a relationship, this follows from the previous observation.

Also consider that popular culture may teach dependencies where there are none.

An associate of mine once advised me not to plan on marrying an attractive woman (I hadn’t asked for his advice, but he gave it anyway). His logic was as follows:

  • Not everyone is attractive;
  • Everyone wants to be in a relationship;
  • Therefore, not everyone can be in a relationship with someone attractive.

He was dating a small-time Russian actress at the time. Naturally.

Putting aside the question of what he might have been implying about me (ಠ╭╮ಠ), I find a couple of flaws in his reasoning. I’ll leave it to the reader to decide what they are.

One idea I dislike a lot in popular culture is the failure to distinguish between attraction and love. In fact, it seems like novels and movies in the Western hemisphere are so fixated on attraction that they risk forgetting love entirely. Is it because attraction is more exciting? I don’t mean to imply that people should, by and large, attempt to fall in love with people they aren’t attracted to. But I do mean that people shouldn’t mistake every passing crush for something meaningful. And the whole “follow your heart” schmaltz is just bad advice if it means making decisions based on one’s hormone cocktail du jour.

I understand that attraction is a useful shortcut for love when you’re limited to a few hundred pages and an easily-bored reader. But it’s important to recognize the effect this shortcut has on people’s ideas and expectations.

One consequence of this conflation is that many of the stories we’re told follow an “attraction ➡️ physical intimacy” arc, which is also the formula for sexual harrassment and assault. There are a lot of factors at play here, and no one is responsible for anyone else’s actions, but I submit that the storytellers of the world have contributed to the sexual harrassment problem by glossing over the intermediate steps.

Questions

If you had to choose three of the four pillars to survive on for the rest of your life, which ones would you choose? Which ones do you think most other people would choose? If a poll were taken, do you hypothesize that the results would differ based on gender, sexuality, age, culture, and/or religion?

Suppose the four pillars were available for purchase on the stock exchange. Which one(s) would you expect to be undervalued? Or overvalued?
