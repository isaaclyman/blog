On users

And why they're at odds with software

Posted on Nov 22, 2022 4 mins read

The year is 2022. We are fish in an ocean of software.

When you visit a website on your phone, you are using software inside of software inside of software (inside of more software? The number of layers is debatable). It’s app-ception. The only things you’re allowed to do in an app are ones the developer has explicitly and painstakingly imagined, created, tested and delivered to you. The software is a vehicle to some desirable end, but more like a train than a bicycle: the destinations are pre-selected and you have no say in the route. In the material world you can move things around and invent new ways to be. In the world of the computer you act within the constraints of a shockingly specific magic spell. The two partial exceptions to this, if there are any, are Microsoft Excel and Minecraft.

Software developers often feel at odds with their users. The feeling is mutual. Using software isn’t something people want to do. People want to complete tasks and have fun. Software happens to be the cheapest way to accomplish those things. To be clear, the popularity of software is not primarily because of its speed or accuracy. It’s because computers are willing to work without pay.

Software developers transform intent into code and then are frustrated when users interact with the code in a way that contradicts their intent. The message has been lost in translation. Users bring their own intent to the code and are frustrated when it flouts their expectations. In this way, the connection between developer and user is very much a human relationship. Software is incidental to the way we misunderstand each other.

Much of the software industry is built around alleviating this misunderstanding. UI and UX designers, product owners, project managers, technical writers, quality assurance testers, even marketers and salespeople to some extent are purposed with carrying messages intact from users to developers and back again. Why should there be such an extraordinary cost to this? What is it about software that makes it defeat its own purposes in the absence of massive guardrails?

We can answer this question by giving some consideration to the typical software user—not a developer, not even someone in the tech industry, but someone who imagines code to be an impenetrable and arcane wall of symbols, someone who uses software the way you use toilet paper: quickly and with a vague feeling of discomfort.

Users are scared

In the 90s, users were scared of losing their work if they forgot to click Save. In the 2000s, they were scared of applications crashing and corrupting their files. In the 2010s, they were scared of getting hacked. It’s too early to say for sure what the prevailing fears of the 2020s will be, but what with the dominance of mobile chipsets and touch screens, I suspect the answer is along the lines of “keyboard shortcuts and menu bars.”

One constant through the decades has been the fear of getting left behind. This is a major reason why the technology adoption curve has a downslope instead of a cliff. People reluctantly come along because the alternative is to live in a world that becomes more foreign and isolating by the day.

Even in a relatively enlightened era of software development where we study the Agile manifesto and label ourselves as “customer-obsessed,” most often it’s the far left side of the curve we’re focused on. Our earliest users tell us what they like and dislike and we cater our development roadmap to them. These users may represent a substantial slice of our future customer base, but by showing up so soon they prove they don’t represent the whole pie. How often do we seek out reluctant software users for our UX tests?

In any case, all but the earliest adopters react to new applications with at least as much fear as excitement. We’ve never taught them not to. A few misplaced taps on their iPhone can leave their door unlocked or turn their thermostat all the way down. Clicking a button with one word on it can result in a thousand-dollar charge to their bank account; we’ve optimized out any opportunity for them to reconsider. Tapping on something that doesn’t even look like a button can open a modal they don’t know how to close.

You may write these off as examples of technical illiteracy, but that’s not fair. You and I have both ended up in situations where we didn’t know what an app was doing or how to make it stop. We’ve spent minutes hunting through UI for a button that should have been easy to spot.

  • Users are scared
  • Users feel dumb
  • Users have little control
    • We let users leave bad reviews for their benefit, because they don’t have actual agency
