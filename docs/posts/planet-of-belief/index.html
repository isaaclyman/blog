Belief

On belief, performance, and effect

Posted on Nov 30, 2023 6 mins read

What do people actually mean when they say they “believe” a particular religion? There are likely many factions we incorrectly group as one because they use the same term.

Imagine you’ve set out on a space journey as humanity’s first envoy to the stars. You arrive on an inhabited planet and meet the locals. As soon as you’ve worked out mutual communication, the first thing you discover is their religious beliefs mirror yours exactly. Not just by analogy or curious resemblance—they hold each of your beliefs, word-for-word, as literal truth. Whatever you are (Protestant, Muslim, Catholic, Mormon, humanist) that’s what they are, too, and have been for generations.

Of course this brings you no particular satisfaction. You already believed what you believe. Why should the beliefs of aliens mean anything to you? (I kid, I kid.)

They’re quick to warn you about a group of dissidents on the same planet: a cult whose beliefs are bizarre and defy the most basic facts of reality. It’s anyone’s guess how they came by the pile of nonsense they call “scripture” or the mortifying theatrics they consider holy rituals.

This was always a one-way trip, so you have to make a choice: who do you live out your days with? At first you think you’d be content to live with those who believe as you do. But this parable hasn’t yet eaten its fill.

As the days pass, you discover the like-minded believers you first encountered are despicable beings. They lie, cheat, bully, steal, and murder. The way they behave horrifies you, and not just because of your beliefs; it’s chilling to the soul of your humanity.

You also discover, through several chance interactions, that the “cult” you were warned about is unwaveringly kind, honest, and gentle. While your fellow believers are killing each other in the streets, the heretics are building hospitals, tending to their families and baking bread.

Do you still prefer the company of your own?

To be clear, I want you to choose between the two. Rejecting both groups and living on your own isn’t a choice. Whoever you put down roots with, you’ll have to tolerate them as they are.

If your answer is “I would try to convert the nonbelievers,” that’s a little embarrassing. Why wouldn’t you spend that time reforming the believers?

To restate the question, do you prefer to stand with who is right (in your view) or who is good?

When you’ve had to make this choice in your own life, what have you actually chosen?

It may seem like I’ve made the decision too obvious or tried to bias you with my word choice. I don’t think I have. We’re surrounded by people who live out opposite sides of this dichotomy, and there have been thinkers throughout history who have argued seriously for both.

It’s necessary that we separate “right” from “good” and consider them apart. Many have disavowed their religion upon determining, to their own satisfaction, that its essential truth claims are false. They’ll tell you that even if they thought religion had a positive effect on the world, it would be insufficient justification for a lie. Others have left religion in protest of religiously-motivated wars and atrocities. To them, even if it was true, that wouldn’t excuse its long and bloody history. The typical atheist perceives religion as neither right nor good, but it’s insightful to ask which is more important.

Too often we talk about these as if they’re the same thing. Jesus is recorded as saying “by their fruits ye shall know them,” and some take this to mean that everything good is also true—as Keats wrote, “Beauty is truth, truth beauty,—that is all / Ye know on earth, and all ye need to know.” I’ve always taken these lines to be tongue-in-cheek (Keats quotes them as being spoken by an urn, not in his own voice), but most critics take them at face value, and they’re some of the most vehemently criticized lines he ever wrote. In any case, it’s fair to point out that sometimes beauty is only an illusion, and sometimes the truth is devastating and horrible.

Let’s also consider those who, of their own free will, remain religious as adults. “Good” and “right” hold uneven priority for them, too. I submit that in every major world religion there are at least three factions: one that literally believes its truth claims, a second that effectively disregards its truth claims but still engages because of its positive (or perhaps just useful) effect on the world, and a third faction, equal to the other two, that stays because it’s comfortable or enjoyable to them personally. In reality many believers are likely a mix of the three, but describing them as “factions” is a useful model.

The first faction is what we typically think of as “religious.” A Christian of this faction believes there is an actual person called God who literally took the form of a man, performed miracles, died, and came back to life. This is an essential part of their day-to-day perspective; the world without God hovering over it just isn’t an image in their mind.

The second faction aligns better with the way we think of politics. It’s less important to a second-faction Christian whether water was ever turned into wine; what matters is that modern Christianity enacts a set of outcomes in the world, such as the promotion of familial loyalty and the avoidance of nihilism. In some cases, it may even be enough that Christianity excludes a few groups who make them uncomfortable.

The third faction only considers literal truth or effect when their beliefs are challenged; the rest of the time they’re religious simply because they’re content to be so. I expect this constitutes a majority of religious people, though less than it used to. Today’s world is interconnected to an extent previous generations couldn’t have dreamed of. People are exposed to new ideas and perspectives every day.
