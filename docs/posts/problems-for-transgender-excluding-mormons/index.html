Problems for transgender-excluding Mormons

Posted on Nov 17, 2023 11 mins read

I grew up in the Mormon church, performed Mormon missionary service, and studied at a Mormon university. Though I no longer identify with the church, my understanding of Mormonism is not dysfunctional.

Many or most Mormons see their belief system as incompatible with transgender acceptance. On the contrary, I believe the Mormon church is well-positioned, perhaps better than any other Christian sect, to understand and include transgender people.

For those who are reluctant to allow transgender experiences into their belief system, I present a few problems that any transgender-excluding Mormon must resolve.

1: The problem of eternal gender

The Family: A Proclamation to the World is a document considered doctrine by many Mormons. It includes the following line:

Gender is an essential characteristic of individual premortal, mortal, and eternal identity and purpose.

In 1995, when this address was given, “gender” and “sex” were broadly considered synonymous (and, outside of academia, often still are).

Since Mormons believe the human spirit has no body in its premortal state, this sentence necessarily means that human spirits can be categorized by sex, i.e. some spirits are male and some spirits are female. The church claims that “gender” here is intended to mean “biological sex at birth,” but the inclusion of the word “premortal” in the Proclamation makes this contradictory. If gender is had before birth, then it is spiritual.

Does God guarantee that every male spirit is born into a male body and every female spirit is born into a female body? In a moment I’ll give evidence that this isn’t the case, nor is it even possible. But putting that aside, there’s an essential belief statement here: that importance is assigned to the sex of one’s spirit first and foremost, and secondarily to the sex (or sexual morphology) of the body. The separation of spiritual and physical sex is often used to argue against transgender acceptance (in the form “that’s not what you really are”) but scripture warns us that “man looketh on the outward appearance, but the Lord looketh on the heart” (1 Samuel 16:7). To deny someone’s internal experience based on their physical traits, then, is backward. If one claims that human spirits are sexed, and one acknowledges that the spirit is not visible to the mortal eye, then it must be impossible to determine one’s spiritual sex by observing their physical body. The two are distinct.

If it were theoretically possible for a male spirit to inhabit a female body (or vice versa), and if the spirit could recognize and know itself with or without the body, then doctrinal logic would have to justify and acknowledge transgender experiences.

The “wrong body” theory of transgender experience is often criticized, and for good reason, but it’s valuable here as an accessible starting point for exploration of gender issues in Mormonism.

2: The problem of human error

Sex is assigned at birth—and often before then. Sexual traits are more difficult to observe in ultrasound, so a technician might spend a few minutes observing a fetus, but the OBGYN announces “it’s a boy” or “it’s a girl” from little more than a glance between the child’s legs. They do this thousands of times over the course of the average career. Over 3.6 million babies are born each year in the United States alone. What are the chances doctors have a 100% success rate determining a baby’s sex? This would be astounding, since doctors (not to mention the rest of us) don’t have a 100% success rate at anything—and that’s including things they put a lot more time and effort into. We respect and appreciate the expertise of doctors, but we shouldn’t expect perfection from them.

The Book of Mormon states that “cursed is he that putteth his trust in the arm of flesh” (2 Nephi 4:34). To say some babies are incorrectly assigned isn’t to accuse God of making mistakes, but rather to acknowledge that humans are imperfect and the human body is marvelously complex. It’s to accept the nature of a fallen world.

If “the worth of souls is great in the sight of God” (D&C 18:10), then even one child with an incorrect gender assignment would be worthy of our consideration. But there are likely far more than that. Even if we could say 99% of babies are correctly assigned, or 99.9%, you would encounter people who are incorrectly assigned all the time. You’d probably know a couple of them by name. It stands to reason they shouldn’t be expected to live their entire lives as something they’re not, just because a human doctor made an error in judgment.

3: The subject of intersex people

As many as 1 in 2,000 people are born with completely ambiguous genitals. That is, their sex cannot be determined from an inspection of their body at birth. In a population the size of the United States, we would expect anywhere from 74,000 to 168,000 people to be in this group.

As many as 1 in 500 newborns receive “corrective” genital surgery. This includes the former group as well as babies whose sex can be determined by a doctor but still doesn’t appear “male” or “female” to the untrained eye. In the U.S., we’d expect between 335,000 and 672,000 people to be in this group.

There are far more people—as many as 2%—who don’t match the strict medical definition of “male” or “female” for other reasons including “chromosome composition, gonadal structure, hormone levels, and the structure of the internal genital duct systems and external genitalia” (ibid.). In the U.S., we’d expect up to 6.7 million people to be in this group—more than the populations of Los Angeles and Chicago combined. And these are just the people documented in medical literature. The actual number could be even larger, because some differences aren’t immediately obvious. Take, for example, the Colorado resident who lived as a man until her 60s, when she got an ultrasound for a kidney stone and was informed she had female sex organs on the inside. Or the 37-year-old British man who found out during an MRI that he had a functioning uterus.

However you consider these groups, it’s clear that we can’t categorize all bodies as completely male or completely female. This leads us to two undeniable conclusions:

  1. If all spirits are male or female and some bodies are neither, then spiritual sex and physical sex aren’t the same thing.
  2. It’s impossible to determine a person’s spiritual sex from the appearance of their body, since many bodies don’t have a specific sex.

Some people think chromosome testing is an unambiguous way to determine if someone’s body is male or female (i.e. males have the XY chromosome, females have the XX chromosome). But most babies don’t receive chromosome testing, and even if they did, many wouldn’t fit into the XX or XY categories. Some people have XXY chromosomes, XYY, XXX, or X. And some people have XY chromosomes but completely female sex characteristics due to androgen insensitivity. Chromosomes aren’t a reliable marker of sex.

In fact, there isn’t any way of defining binary, male-or-female sex that includes all human beings.

If you exclude transgender people from faith communities because their presentation doesn’t match their assigned sex, do you also exclude people with some level of chromosomal, hormonal, or morphological variation from the norm? How would you even know who these people are? Do you exclude intersex people, who may feel strongly that they’re men, women, or neither, with no physical exam that can prove them right or wrong? Do you devise hundreds of complex rules for determining a person’s “real sex,” then update them every time a new variation in the human body is discovered? It should be clear that all of these are untenable.

The Mormon church does not believe in original sin. They believe the only factors in a person’s salvation are their own free choices and the Atonement of Christ. There is obviously no room for the denial of intersex people in the church (nor should there be anywhere else) which means either the idea of binary spiritual gender or the idea of direct correlation between physical and spiritual gender is incorrect.

4: The problem of doctrinal voids around gender

For now, let’s define “sex” as a person’s physical sex traits and “gender” as everything else we expect depending on whether we see them as a man or a woman. For example, Western women are stereotypically associated with long hair, makeup, jewelry, skirts and blouses, raising children, personality traits like “nurturing” and “altruistic,” and careers like nurse and secretary. Western men are stereotypically associated with facial hair, neckties, working outside the home, personality traits like “ambitious” and “aggressive,” and careers like doctor and CEO. Without a long look at history, it’s impossible to understand how the infinite possibilities of a person’s life came to be sorted into just two categories, and how so many of us came to believe those categories are meaningful.

Gender is just a horoscope based on bodies instead of constellations. Clothing, grooming, personality and career have nothing to do with genitals, but we’ve bundled them up all the same. In academia, it’s said that gender is “constructed”—that is, it’s a set of cultural ideas, not something physical and essential.

A few Mormon leaders have encouraged conformity to gender stereotypes (specifically when it comes to piercings, hair length, and leadership in the family). However, recent changes to the church’s dress and appearance guidelines for youth, as well as the temple ceremony, suggest that these specifics are not doctrinal.

The “gender” of clothing and appearance depend entirely on your cultural environment. 10th-century Persian cavalrymen were the first to wear high heels. Roman gladiators wore skirts and men in several Eastern countries still do. Makeup was originally worn by Ancient Egyptian men and later by Roman men. The expectations of 21st-century Westerners with regard to gendered appearance were mostly invented in France and England only a couple of centuries ago. Even “he” and “she” pronouns are cultural; several languages don’t have gendered pronouns at all.

There are, of course, historical examples of women wearing clothing that would come to be considered stereotypically male. But these examples aren’t as surprising to modern readers, since nearly all types of clothing are considered acceptable wear for 21st-century Western women.

The Mormon church forbids members from presenting as anything other than their assigned sex at birth. When someone talks about “presenting” as a particular sex, it’s safe to assume they’re talking about gender presentation—makeup, dress, and grooming—not the presentation of their genitals. But since Mormon leaders insist that “sex” and “gender” are synonyms, it’s only fair to ask: do they believe that all of Western civilization has undergone sex transition multiple times throughout history? Cultural ideas about men and women are a moving target and have been for a long time. The most masculine-presenting men from Ancient Egyptian or 17th-century French aristocracy would appear feminine-presenting or perhaps androgynous to us, if we evaluated them by modern standards.

By punishing Mormons who transition, even just in dress and grooming, the church declares gender presentation to be a moral issue. The more one thinks about this, the stranger it becomes. What is it about the shape of one’s clothing, the style of one’s hair, or the application of mascara that is fundamentally moral? Why should a triangle be more morally feminine than a square? Even pronouns are mysterious: what about the word “she” is part of god’s design for women?

And what about intersex people? Church leaders must wish for them to present as androgynous—for the sake of consistency—but the situation has never been addressed.

5: The problem of religious compatibility

Those exploring a gender transition are only trying to determine the nature of their spirit: the ideas and traits that make them feel in tune with their true self. This soul-searching, this courageous transformation from the old self to the new, is celebrated in other contexts within Mormonism.

Transgender experiences align neatly with Mormon cultural ideals. The transformation of the body is a theme that persists throughout Mormon scripture (Moses 1:11; 3 Nephi 28:15). Social transition could be seen as a deepening of conversion and a commitment to one’s divine nature (Alma 23:16-17; Genesis 32:28). Medical transition could be seen as denying the fallen nature of the body and enacting a metaphor for the resurrection (Mosiah 3:19; Mosiah 16:10). The full inclusion of transgender people in all ordinances would be a demonstration that “black and white, bond and free, male and female […] all are alike unto God” (2 Nephi 26:33). Much like today, very few people would transition or even want to, but those people could be celebrated in the same way as those who are baptized or receive temple ordinances.

By what doctrine or scripture does the church deny transgender people? There’s no discussion of the topic in the Standard Works. The founder of the church’s Young Men’s program was a drag queen, and we have no record of him ever being punished for it. Throughout the 19th century, there was no preaching on the topic. The exclusion of transgender and gender non-conforming people is a modern phenomenon, and a baffling one. To exclude transgender people, the church had to publish new teachings that didn’t match the old; to include them, they would have needed very little.

Even core church doctrines are subject to change. Famously, Black people are now permitted to hold the priesthood in the Mormon church, following decades of preaching that it would never happen.
