Your brain is not your own

On will, biology, and trying harder

Posted on Nov 15, 2021 4 mins read

A cursory look at this post may give the impression that I’m arguing against the existence of free will. I will not be doing that today, first because it sounds boring, and second because I fundamentally do believe in free will (for pragmatic reasons, at the very least).

What I do want to argue today is that, despite a growing body of evidence, most people routinely:

  • overestimate the role of will (both free will and willpower) in their lives
  • underestimate the activity of their subconscious mind and the extent to which its underlying biology can change

You might consider your life on two different axes, one precise and one general. The axis of events includes moments such as the beginning or end of a relationship, the birth of a child, or a career achievement. The axis of outcomes includes ongoing concerns such as the amount of money you make, your physical health and fitness, the way you think you look, and your self-worth.

Of course, this geometry falls apart under scrutiny. There is a little overlap and a lot of interaction between these axes. Events are often the inflection point at which outcomes change, and while events are more capricious and unpredictable than outcomes, outcomes may also lead to events.

Most people want to exert some measure of control over this process. Thus the concept of the decision, a moment during which a person consciously enacts effort within their sphere of control toward one of multiple opportunities. Bargain-brand philosophers love to tell us that we make hundreds of decisions each day, but this trivializes decisions so much as to make them absurd. Decisions must require both consideration and cognitive difficulty or they are moot. For example, you are capable of conscious control over your breathing, but it does not follow that you make a decision every time you breathe. Typically you delegate that process to your subconscious and forget about it completely. There is no consideration and no difficulty. Neither do you decide whether to go to work most days, assuming you are moderately happy with your employment situation and haven’t been sick. It may be hard or unpleasant to get out of bed and attend to your routine, but it is assumed you will show up to work, and an assumption is not a decision. Most of our actions are in one of these categories: delegate processes managed automatically, like breathing, or assumptions so quickly rubber-stamped by our conscious mind that we can’t call them decisions without making the term meaningless. There is also a category of action where we may stop and consider the alternatives, but there is no real consequence or emotional weight attached so any choice we make is unimportant. Choosing from the menu at a restaurant, for example, or deciding what day to meet a friend. Decisions like these are usually made on an emotional basis, and emotions are one of the subconscious mind’s preferred mechanisms of control.

Studies find that most of our brain activity happens outside of our awareness. Dr. David Eagleman, neuroscience professor at Stanford, estimates that 95% of our “decisions” are made by our subconscious. Of the remaining 5%, as many as half may be unintentional, the result of “background noise.” And the decisions we’re absolutely, positively sure we’ve made consciously have probably been predicted and prepared by our subconscious mind several seconds in advance.

Even the information we receive from our senses—the most rational basis for a decision—is quietly micromanaged by our subconscious. Our brain filters out the majority of the stimuli our senses take in, allowing us to focus on whatever our biology tells us is most important.

In short: your conscious mind thinks it is “you.” But as far as we’re able to tell, it is not. Like Alfredo Linguini, the clumsy young chef in Pixar’s Ratatouille, it pretends everything we do is intentional while the real mastermind sits invisibly under our cap.
